Thrust Vectoring Aerospike Rocket Nozzle

In Fall 2021, a team of 4 from the Boston University Rocket Propulsion Group including myself proposed the independent project to test an aerospike nozzle for our two-semester senior design capstone course. Using experience from BURPG and internships, the team plans to test a variety of test stand designs to achieve thrust vector control through fluid flow manipulation; doing so would greatly increase the viability of aerospike engines in industry. To simplify the cost of the student project, the team plans to use inert gas flows to simulate propellants and 3D print the nozzle geometries. Having the most fluids experience from BURPG and internships, I am serving as the lead fluids engineer to design, construct, and operate the test stand for the thruster. I designed the initial P&ID as a conceptual test stand design, which will simulate propellant feed into the aerospike thruster using inert nitrogen gas. the team will use this base test stand to conduct baseline testing of a non-TVC thruster before adding the thrust vector upgrades that are researched and developed.

Once the calculations made to size the thrusters are verified to give nominal performance, thrust vector control elements will be installed into the test stand. The proposed solution is to use fluidic injection through the spike to impinge on the nozzle flow and create a disturbance that will redirect the flow outwards, pitching the engine thrust in the other direction. A series of three load cells will be used providing data acquisition on the thrust in all three axes.

The aerospike thruster was designed by myself using an open-source Python code that generates nozzle geometry when upstream conditions and desired performance are inputted. The code was written by research students at MIT working on aerospike engines, and was based on a research paper that derived equations from FORTRAN programs used by NASA to design plug nozzles. For the team's nozzle, upstream conditions are 125 psig of pure nitrogen at room temperature, with an expected thrust of 30 lbf. The dimensions were used to model the nozzle in Solidworks, with a 75% truncation of the spike.

This design is pending computational fluid dynamics analysis to verify the calculated performance of the engine, as well as CFD analysis into the ideal position on the spike to impinge the flow from. Once the CFD is complete, the nozzle will be printed and the components for the test stand will be ordered for assembly.
